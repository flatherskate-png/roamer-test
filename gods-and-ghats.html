India does not ease you in. It arrives all at once — the smell, the noise, the colour, the scale — and asks you to recalibrate everything you thought you knew about how a city moves. This route traces the old Mughal heartland from Delhi's chaos to Kathmandu's thin mountain air, through pink cities, marble mausoleums, sacred rivers, and labyrinthine old towns that predate every empire that tried to claim them.

The Route

Six stops.
Two countries. Countless lifetimes.

01
Stop 01 · Gateway

Delhi, India

Eight cities built on top of each other, and all of them are still arguing.

Delhi is not one place but a palimpsest — Mughal mosques beside colonial boulevards beside gleaming metro stations beside crumbling medieval walls. Old Delhi's lanes around Chandni Chowk are among the most sensory-overwhelming streets on earth. New Delhi's wide Lutyens avenues feel like a different country entirely. Give yourself three days minimum: one for old, one for new, one for recovery.

02
Stop 02 · Rajasthan

Jaipur, Rajasthan

A city painted pink by royal decree in 1876. It has not reconsidered.

The Pink City earns its nickname — the old walled quarter is genuinely, uniformly terracotta-rose, a colour chosen by Maharaja Ram Singh II to welcome the Prince of Wales. The Amber Fort sits on a ridge above the city and requires a full morning. The Hawa Mahal's 953-windowed facade is best seen at sunrise before the tuk-tuks arrive. The bazaars sell textiles, gems, and blue pottery; buy something. You'll wish you had.

03
Stop 03 · Uttar Pradesh

Agra, India

You have seen ten thousand photographs of it. None of them prepared you.

The Taj Mahal is not overrated. This is worth saying clearly, because nothing that famous is supposed to be this good. Shah Jahan built it as a tomb for his wife Mumtaz Mahal, who died in 1631 during the birth of their fourteenth child. It took 22 years and 20,000 workers. See it at sunrise. See it again at sunset if you can. Agra Fort — a few kilometres away — is also extraordinary and receives a fraction of the crowds.

04
Stop 04 · City of Nawabs

Lucknow, India

The city that turned refinement into an art form and never stopped.

Lucknow was the capital of the Nawabs of Awadh — a court culture so devoted to etiquette, cuisine, and the arts that it persists visibly today. The Bara Imambara is an 18th-century mosque complex with a labyrinthine upper floor — the bhul-bhulaiya, a genuine maze — that is disorienting and wonderful. The old Chowk bazaar sells chikankari embroidery produced here for centuries. The food is a destination in itself: Lucknow's biryani and kakori kebabs are among the finest in northern India.

05
Stop 05 · The Eternal City

Varanasi, Uttar Pradesh

One of the oldest continuously inhabited cities on earth. It has seen everything and is surprised by nothing.

Varanasi is where Hindus come to die — and to be reborn, and to bathe, and to pray, and to burn incense, and to argue philosophy. The ghats are stone steps descending to the Ganges, and at dawn the river fills with pilgrims, priests, boats, smoke, flowers, and an atmosphere impossible to describe and unforgettable to witness. The evening Ganga Aarti fire ceremony at Dashashwamedh Ghat is one of the great spectacles of the subcontinent. Sarnath — where the Buddha gave his first sermon — is just 13 km away and an easy half-day.

06
Stop 06 · Journey's End

Kathmandu, Nepal

The air is thinner here. So is the boundary between the everyday and the sacred.

Kathmandu sits in a valley ringed by Himalayan peaks and filled with temples, stupas, and alleyways that smell of incense and marigolds. Pashupatinath — the holiest Hindu temple in Nepal — sits on the Bagmati River and hosts cremations on its ghats, a more intimate echo of Varanasi. Swayambhunath, the Monkey Temple, watches the city from a forested hill. Thamel is chaotic and touristy and useful; the old city of Patan across the river is quieter and more beautiful.

Before you go.

When to Go

October through March is the window. The monsoon runs June–September and makes road travel miserable. November and February are sweet spots — cool, clear, manageable crowds. Delhi in May reaches 45°C; this is not a summer trip.

Getting Around

Indian Railways is excellent between major cities and vastly preferable to road for long legs. Book AC 2-tier or 3-tier sleeper for overnight journeys. Hire a driver for day trips — worth every rupee. For Varanasi to Kathmandu, flying is strongly recommended.

The Golden Triangle

Delhi–Jaipur–Agra is a loop, not a line. Jaipur is southwest of Delhi; Agra is southeast. The route backtracks northeast from Jaipur to Agra before heading east again. Standard and expected — just don't read it as a straight line.

Visas

India e-visas are available online for most nationalities — allow 2–4 business days. Nepal visa on arrival at Tribhuvan Airport and the Sunauli land border. Keep separate USD cash for the Nepal visa fee.

The Pace Question

India has a way of consuming days you were sure you had. Three weeks is the minimum for this route done properly; four is comfortable. Varanasi alone will steal two days if you let it — which you should.

What to Expect

Sensory overload is not a side effect — it is the experience. The noise, the colour, the chaos of traffic and vendors and pilgrims are not obstacles between you and India; they are India. Lean in rather than away.
