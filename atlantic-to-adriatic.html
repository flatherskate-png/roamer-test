London to Dubrovnik by rail is one of those trips that sounds like a fantasy until you actually price it out and realize it's entirely doable. Interrail passes still exist, bless them, and the route practically plans itself — Paris, the Swiss Alps, Venice, then Croatia's walled city on the Adriatic, which was so photogenic it doubled as King's Landing. Three to four weeks is civilized. Two is possible but you'll spend the flight home making a list of everything you missed.

The Route

Six stops.
One continent, coast to coast.

01
Stop 01 · United Kingdom

London, United Kingdom

The city that invented the idea of a city and has been arguing with itself about it ever since.

London is where the route begins and where most people spend too little time — three days minimum, four is better. The British Museum alone is a full day if you let it be. The neighborhoods reward wandering: Shoreditch and Bermondsey for food and art, South Bank for the river, Notting Hill on a Saturday morning when the market is running. St. Paul's from the Millennium Bridge at dusk. The Tube is a miracle. Use it.

02
Stop 02 · Netherlands

Amsterdam, Netherlands

A city built on water and compromise, which turns out to be a surprisingly durable combination.

Amsterdam's 165 canals and 1,500 bridges create a city that is best understood by getting lost in it, preferably by bicycle. The Rijksmuseum and the Van Gogh Museum are essential; the Anne Frank House requires booking weeks in advance. The Jordaan neighbourhood — narrow streets, brown cafes, independent bookshops — is where you'll spend more time than planned. The city is smaller than it feels and more complex than it looks.

03
Stop 03 · France

Paris, France

It really is that beautiful. This is annoying but true.

Paris is the most written-about city on earth for a reason that becomes obvious within the first hour: it is simply, almost unfairly, beautiful. The Haussmann boulevards, the river, the light on the stone in the late afternoon — it looks like a painting because generations of painters came here to paint it. The Louvre is too large to do properly in a day; pick two or three rooms and go deep. The Musée d'Orsay fits in a morning and is, for money, the finest art museum in Europe. Eat everything.

04
Stop 04 · Switzerland

Interlaken, Switzerland

Between two lakes, beneath three peaks, at the point where Europe's scenery stops being subtle.

Interlaken sits between Lakes Thun and Brienz with the Eiger, Mönch, and Jungfrau rising behind it — a setting that feels like it was designed by someone who had run out of restraint. The Jungfraujoch railway climbs to 3,454 metres, which is the highest train station in Europe and a legitimate engineering marvel. In summer: hiking, paragliding, kayaking. In winter: some of the finest skiing in the Alps. The town itself is small and tourist-oriented but cheerfully so; the scenery around it is the destination.

05
Stop 05 · Italy

Venice, Italy

A city built in the sea to survive its enemies, which outlasted them all, and is now slowly returning to the water.

Venice is genuinely unlike anywhere else — 118 islands connected by 400 bridges, no cars, no straight lines, and a navigational logic that defeats everyone on the first day. Walk away from St Mark's Square as quickly as possible into Dorsoduro and Castello, where the city belongs to its residents rather than its visitors. Take a vaporetto down the Grand Canal at dusk. Eat cicchetti at a bacaro. Stay at least two nights and accept that you will get lost, repeatedly, and that this is the correct outcome.

06
Stop 06 · Journey's End

Dubrovnik, Croatia

A walled medieval city on an Adriatic cliff so photogenic they built a fantasy kingdom out of it.

Dubrovnik's old city is encircled by 13th-century walls that you can walk in their entirety — two kilometres, an hour, and one of the great urban experiences in Europe. The limestone streets inside are polished to a high shine by centuries of feet. The Adriatic below is the colour it is in photographs, which is to say improbably blue. Game of Thrones filmed here extensively, which brought crowds that are manageable in shoulder season and overwhelming in August. Come in May or October.

Before you go.

The Rail Question

An Interrail Global Pass covers all train legs on this route except the Eurostar (London–Brussels–Amsterdam), which requires a separate booking. Buy Eurostar tickets early — they sell out and prices rise sharply. Night trains exist on some legs and are worth considering for Venice.

Venice to Dubrovnik

This is the one leg that doesn't have a clean direct train. Options: drive the Croatian coast (spectacular), take the overnight ferry from Venice to Split then bus or drive south, or fly. Most travelers who've done the whole route overland consider the coastal drive a highlight.

When to Go

May–June and September–October are the sweet spots: good weather, manageable crowds, reasonable prices. July–August is hot, crowded, and expensive everywhere except London. Dubrovnik in August is genuinely overwhelming. Interlaken in winter is a different and equally valid trip.

How Long

Two weeks is possible but leaves you wishing for more everywhere. Three weeks is comfortable. Four weeks lets you take the detours — Burgundy, Lake Como, Split, Plitvice — that make the route rather than just the stops. The Interrail pass rewards the longer version.

Booking Ahead

The Anne Frank House in Amsterdam books out weeks in advance — do it first. The Jungfraujoch railway fills up in peak season. The Last Supper in Milan requires advance reservations. Everything else is walkable, but these three will cost you the trip if you leave them to chance.

Crowds

This route passes through some of the most visited places in Europe. The difference between peak and shoulder season is not minor — it is the difference between a good trip and a great one. If you have any flexibility at all, avoid the school holidays.
